Can Question-Decomposition Prompting Bridge the Long-Context NLI Reasoning Gap?

A working explainer of the project, the methods being compared, the models under test, and where things stand right now — written to give everyone enough context to write the report without re-deriving it from scratch.

Overview

This project replicates and extends Liu et al. (2020/2021), Natural Language Inference in Context — the paper that introduced the ConTRoL dataset — by testing whether modern open-weight LLMs, prompted (not fine-tuned) in different ways, can close the gap on long-document contextual reasoning that the original paper identified as a hard problem for NLI systems.

The core idea across every method we test (except the two simplest baselines): instead of asking a model to read a long passage and a claim and just guess Entailment / Contradiction / Neutral in one shot, break the problem into smaller sub-questions first, find the answers to those sub-questions inside the passage, and only then decide the final label from that gathered evidence. The methods differ in where the sub-questions come from and how the gathered evidence gets combined — that's the whole design space this project explores.

Dataset — ConTRoL

ConTRoL (Contextual Textual Entailment Robustness with Long texts) contains 8,325 expert-designed premise/hypothesis pairs. Each premise is a genuinely long passage (~500 words) — not a single sentence like most classic NLI datasets (SNLI, MNLI) — and each hypothesis is a short claim about it. The task: label the relationship as Entailment, Contradiction, or Neutral.

Why this matters: long premises require genuine multi-step reasoning — connecting facts that appear in different sentences, sometimes doing simple arithmetic or comparisons across separated pieces of text — which is exactly the kind of reasoning single-shot prompting tends to fail at.

Methods

Six methods total. The first two are simple baselines with no evidence-gathering step; the rest all share the same underlying machinery (a Locator that finds relevant sentences, an Answer Extractor that reads them, and a Classifier that decides the final label) — they only differ in how their sub-questions get generated.

zero_shotbaseline

The simplest possible approach: premise + hypothesis go straight into one prompt, the model answers directly. No sub-questions, no evidence-gathering. This sets the floor — every other method should beat it.

few_shot_cotbaseline

Same one-shot structure as zero_shot, but with 3 fixed worked examples (one per label) shown first, nudging the model to reason step-by-step before answering. Tests how much simple in-context learning helps, without any decomposition machinery.

retrieve_then_classifycontrol

The key control condition. No sub-questions are generated at all — the Locator searches the premise directly using the hypothesis itself as its search query, pulls out the most relevant sentences, and the classifier reads those raw sentences (not a paraphrase) to decide. This isolates one question: does simply finding the right sentences already explain most of the benefit, or do the generated sub-questions add something on top? Surprisingly, in our diagnostic runs so far, this simple method has outperformed every question-generating method we've re-verified — more on why in "Findings so far."

h_question"hypothesis question"

Generates 2-4 probe questions from the hypothesis — it never reads the premise while forming questions ("hypothesis-aware, premise-blind"). Each question is then answered by searching the premise, and all the answers are combined into a final verdict. The questions are seeded by first pulling "anchor" phrases out of the hypothesis — see the POS vs. SRL comparison below for how those anchors are generated.

Timing: the seeder runs at Stage 0, before any LLM call at all — for pos that's pure NLTK grammar tagging on the hypothesis text (no model call whatsoever); for srl it's one LLM call. Either way, the resulting keyphrases are just context handed into Stage 1's prompt — they don't set how many questions come out.

Is it one question per keyphrase? No. Real numbers from this exact sample: the pos seeder produced seeds = ["Sharks", "groups", "live"] — 3 keyphrases (2 noun phrases + 1 main verb) — but Stage 1 wrote only 2 questions, not 3. h_question's Stage 1 prompt is fixed at "generate 2-4 questions for the hypothesis as a whole" regardless of keyphrase count, and its "information integration" rule actively pushes the model to merge related keyphrases into one combined question rather than writing one question per phrase.

p_question"premise question"

The mirror image of h_question: generates questions from the premise instead of the hypothesis ("premise-aware, hypothesis-blind"). It supports 3 generation modes, run as separate experiments and compared side by side (not one chosen over the others) — this is where POS/SRL show up again, in a different role than in h_question:

  • decomposition (the default, detailed below) — no seeder at all; the LLM reads the whole premise itself and decides how to break it up.
  • seeded — uses the same pos/srl seeders explained earlier, but pointed at the premise instead of the hypothesis: the seeder pulls anchor phrases out of the premise text first, then one question gets generated per anchor.
  • freeform (legacy baseline) — up to 15 free-form questions, no fact/relation distinction at all.

What is atomic decomposition?

It means breaking the premise down into the smallest pieces that can each stand alone as one checkable claim — instead of one big question about the whole paragraph, you get many small, specific ones. Each piece gets tagged as one of two kinds:

fact  one standalone piece of information — a single date, name, number, or event, checkable entirely on its own  ·  relation  a connection between two facts — a comparison ("which was bigger"), a cause ("what led to this"), a ranking, or a contrast — can't be checked by looking at just one isolated sentence

Simplest possible illustration first (made up, not from the dataset — just to build intuition) Made-up premise: "Tom is 10 years old. His sister Ann is 15 years old."
  • fact "How old is Tom?" — check the first sentence alone. Done. No need to look at the second sentence at all.
  • fact "How old is Ann?" — check the second sentence alone. Done. No need to look at the first sentence at all.
  • relation "Who is older, Tom or Ann?" — this one is impossible to answer from either sentence by itself. You need both ages at once, then compare them. That act of connecting two already-known facts is exactly what makes it a "relation" instead of a "fact."

That's the whole rule: if a question can be answered by staring at one single clause in isolation, it's a fact. If answering it requires holding two separate facts in your head at the same time and relating them (comparing, ranking, explaining what caused what, saying what came before what), it's a relation. A relation is never a "new" piece of information on its own — it's always built on top of facts that already exist.

Notice how each fact question maps to exactly one clause of the original sentence (the weather, the water, the relief operations, the garbage) — that's the "atomic" part: the sentence gets cut at every place it could be cut into a smaller, independently-checkable claim. The two relation questions instead ask about how two of those clauses connect to each other (sunshine's effect on the situation; the contrast between two of the facts) — something no single clause alone tells you.

Because it never sees the hypothesis while generating questions, p_question has a confirmed structural weakness: it can't reliably target a comparison the hypothesis cares about unless the premise happens to invite that comparison on its own. Concretely, on a different sample (hypothesis: "Young's talent as a doctor surpassed his other skills"), p_question generated 30 real questions about Young's life and work — none of them tested how his medical skill compared to his other skills, simply because nothing told the model to look for that specific comparison.

p_question's three modes — seeds, facts, and relations, explained end-to-end

The one-line version: p_question has three different modes for turning the premise into questions, and the project runs all three as separate experiments to compare them — not one chosen and the others discarded. Only one mode ever uses a "seed," and only one mode ever produces a "relation" — those two things don't both happen every time; each belongs to a different mode.

The three modes, side by side

All three modes converge afterward into the exact same downstream pipeline (cap the list → locate each question in the premise → answer it → classify). The only thing that differs between modes is how the question list gets built — a single run uses one mode at a time (it's a setting passed in), but the project tests all three side by side as separate experiments rather than combining them into one. (Also worth knowing: freeform exists in the code but isn't part of the current production sweep at all — only decomposition and seeded are actually being run at scale right now.)

So, directly: what is a "seed"?

A seed is a short hint phrase pulled out of the premise before the question-writing call happens — it exists only to give that LLM call something concrete to latch onto, instead of it inventing questions totally freely. It's Step 0, not a question itself, and it only exists in seeded mode. If you're running decomposition mode, there are no seeds at all — the model just reads the premise and writes both fact and relation questions in one go, unaided.

And: when does "relation" happen?

Only in decomposition mode's Step 2, and nowhere else. A relation question is not something that happens "inside" seeding — and SRL's "roles" (agent/theme/location/time) are a completely different concept from a "relation" question, even though both use similar-sounding vocabulary:

SRL role — describes the grammar of one single sentence (who did what, to whom, when) — still just describing one fact.
relation (decomposition mode only) — a connection between two separate facts — "which is bigger," "what caused what," "which came first."

So a p_question run using seeded + srl produces SRL-flavored fact questions but zero relation questions — relation-extraction simply isn't part of that mode's prompt. To get relation questions at all, you have to be in decomposition mode.

SRL seeder (1 LLM call)

Instead of flat keyphrases, SRL asks the model to find each verb (predicate) in the premise and label its PropBank-style roles — who did what, to what, when, where, how. Each seed comes out formatted as "{role}: {span} [predicate: {verb}]" — richer than POS's flat list, but costs one extra LLM call over the whole premise.

bridge_questionboth texts

Designed specifically to fix the blind spot above: it generates questions from both the premise and hypothesis together, the only method that sees both at once during question generation. It's explicitly instructed to identify the hypothesis's core claim first, then guarantee at least one generated question — labeled bridge — tests that claim directly, instead of only asking about related-but-safer side topics.

Real example — full premise, from an actual run (id_5185, "Sharks Face Extinction") Premise (~500 words, scroll to read in full)

POS vs. SRL — the same hypothesis, two seeding styles

Both seeders extract "anchors" from text before questions get generated — POS (Part-of-Speech tagging) is a classic, deterministic NLP technique with no LLM call: just pull out noun phrases and key verbs. SRL (Semantic Role Labeling) uses an LLM call instead, to identify each anchor's actual role in the sentence — who is doing what, to what — not just which words are nouns. Same real sample, run through both:

pos seeder (no LLM call, fast, deterministic) — hypothesis: "Sharks live in groups." Sharks, groups, live — just the key noun phrases and verb, no labels on what role each one plays.
srl seeder (LLM call, structure-aware) — same hypothesis agent Sharks   location in groups   — both tied to the same predicate live

What those role labels mean: agent = who or what is performing the action (here, the sharks doing the "living"); location = where the action happens (here, "in groups" — not just a word, but tagged as specifically describing where/how they live); predicate = the action/verb itself that ties the other roles together (here, "live"). SRL doesn't just list the same words as POS — it explicitly says who is doing what, and in what context, whereas POS gives you the same words with no relationship between them at all. Both seeding styles happened to produce very similar final questions here and both got the sample right — SRL's extra structure is designed to help most on sentences where a flat keyword list would leave the relationship between the anchors ambiguous.

Shared infrastructure: Answer Extractor and Classifier

h_question, p_question, and bridge_question all differ only in where their questions come from — everything downstream of question generation is identical shared code, used the same way by all three:

  • Locator — given the numbered premise and one question, returns up to 5 sentence indices most likely to contain the answer (allows non-adjacent, multi-hop sentences).
  • Answer Extractor ("shared answering" in the code) — reads only those located sentences and writes a concise answer to the question, using nothing else. Returns NOT_ANSWERABLE if the located sentences genuinely don't address the question. This is the step that turns "found the right sentences" into an actual usable answer.
  • Classifier ("shared classifier" in the code) — takes every (question, answer) pair collected for a sample plus the hypothesis, and decides the final Entailment/Contradiction/Neutral label in one call. It's explicitly instructed that one clearly decisive piece of evidence is enough on its own — weaker or unrelated evidence sitting next to it shouldn't "water down" a strong signal.

Because this machinery is fully shared, any accuracy difference between h_question/p_question/bridge_question comes entirely from what questions get asked, not from how evidence gets read or judged — which is what makes them a clean, controlled comparison.

Few-shot examples baked into the prompts

Two different things in this project are called "few-shot," worth telling apart:

  1. few_shot_cot's demonstrations — 3 real dataset samples (one per label) shown in every single call that method ever makes, always the same 3, pulled from the held-out dev split so they're never contaminated by whatever's being tested.
  2. The few-shot toggle on h_question/p_question/bridge_question's Stage 1 — an on/off switch for whether that method's question-generation step sees 2-3 worked examples of "here's a hypothesis/premise, here's the questions a good answer would generate" before writing its own. Models under test

All three are open-weight (not fine-tuned, used exactly as released) large language models, run on self-hosted GPU infrastructure rather than a paid API — chosen to compare how different model families/providers handle the same reasoning task.

What happened before this point

Worth knowing for the report's methodology section: a meaningful chunk of this project's recent work was diagnosing and fixing infrastructure problems that were silently producing bad data, not just running experiments.

  • 1A lab-server connectivity bug. The server address configured in the project pointed at the wrong Tailscale IP (and the wrong URL path), causing every request to silently hang for minutes before failing. This had been misdiagnosed earlier as "the server is just slow/unstable" — it was actually a one-line config error, now fixed.
  • 2A JSON-parsing bug in p_question. The model would occasionally emit a malformed field (a missing quote around a label like "fact"), which silently discarded the entire batch of generated questions for that sample and fell back to a low-quality zero-shot guess — affecting roughly 1 in 7 samples until a small repair step was added to recover them.
  • 3An investigation into why p_question underperforms, which led to discovering the retrieve_then_classify control condition currently outperforms it (see Findings below).
  • 4Three separate attempts to fix p_question's "voting" aggregation mode (a mode where each generated question is judged independently and the majority label wins) — none succeeded; all three landed at roughly the same, worse-than-baseline accuracy. Voting mode is being treated as an open problem, not something to force into this round of results.
  • 5Calibration before committing to full-scale runs. A small timing/token-usage pilot was run first on ~20 samples per method to estimate real costs before committing to the current 1,500-sample-per-model production sweep, given a hard multi-day time budget.

Findings so far

  • 1retrieve_then_classify (no question generation at all) currently beats every re-verified p_question configuration — 70% vs. a best of 58%, on an n=50 diagnostic slice. It also shows better-balanced predictions across all three labels, where p_question tends to over-predict Neutral.
  • 2Why: retrieve_then_classify's Locator sees the hypothesis directly when searching the premise, so it reliably finds the one sentence a comparison hinges on. p_question never sees the hypothesis while generating questions, so it structurally can't target a hypothesis-specific comparison unless the premise happens to invite it on its own — confirmed concretely on a real example (the "Young's medical skill" case above).
  • 3Removing p_question's old top-K question filter helped substantially (54-58% now vs. 24-46% previously) — feeding the classifier the full pool of generated questions instead of a pre-filtered top-3 let its own "one decisive piece of evidence is enough" reasoning actually find the needle, rather than gambling on which 3 questions survived filtering.
  • 4p_question's voting mode looks fundamentally, not superficially, broken. On a real failure case, the one genuinely decisive question was correctly judged — but outvoted 9-to-1 by other questions that were each individually, correctly Neutral (real content, just not relevant to that specific hypothesis). This points at the aggregation mechanism itself, not at which questions get selected.
  • 5At least one dataset label appears to be incorrect — one sample's gold label (Entailment) has no textual support at all in its paired premise, confirmed against the raw dataset file. Some fraction of every method's "wrong" predictions may reflect this kind of label noise rather than a real reasoning failure.
